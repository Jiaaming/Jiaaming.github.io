ğŸ‡¨ğŸ‡¦ Winnipeg

After a short break in early October, I spent a weekend visiting Winnipegâ€”a city frequently mentioned by immigration consultants.

Manitoba, the province where Winnipeg is located, is one of the regions where Indigenous peoples and European colonists first made contact. The city center lies at the confluence of the Red and Assiniboine rivers, an area known as “The Forks,” which has been a place of trade and gathering for various tribes for thousands of years. This is also the birthplace of the MÃ©tis peopleâ€”descendants of Indigenous peoples and French settlers, who are a highly representative group in Canada’s modern multicultural narrative.

Manitoba is also an officially bilingual province, with English and French used side-by-side. However, a third script I often saw on the streets and in museums was unexpected: I initially thought it was some ancient Indigenous language, but it was actually a 19th-century constructed script called “Cree syllabics.” It was created by missionaries to facilitate the spread of religion and education, and today, tens of thousands of Canadians still consider Cree their mother tongue.

The streets of Winnipeg have few pedestrians, and many buildings gave me a unique “Soviet” feelingâ€”heavy and orderly.

This style has nothing to do with ideology but stems from Winnipeg’s deep working-class history. In the early 20th century, the city was known for its labor movement, and the 1919 Winnipeg General Strike was one of the largest labor actions in Canadian history. As a result, the city has dense public housing, and its murals often feature realistic or social themes, which is quite rare in other Canadian cities.

After getting off the plane, my first stop was the Canadian Museum for Human Rights.

The exhibits inside combine artistic installations with written narratives, covering a vast range of topics from the injustices faced by Indigenous peoples to colonialism, slavery, and contemporary issues of gender equality and immigration. However, the museum has also sparked controversy, including criticism for its focus on Western history, the insufficient representation of Indigenous human rights, and significant budget overruns for its construction.

What surprised me was that several entire floors were dedicated to the Holocaust, the genocide of Jews during the Nazi era. It turns out the museum’s creation was primarily driven by the Jewish-Canadian entrepreneur Izzy Asper.

This made me wonder why the Jewish people, who have been persecuted so often throughout history, have managed to maintain such a powerful influence in modern society. GPT provided several common explanations:

  • Forced out of many traditional industries, they had to cultivate expertise in fields that rely on knowledge and critical thinking.
  • Their culture places an extreme emphasis on education, reason, and dialectical thinking.
  • They have a strong, tightly-knit community with traditions of mutual support and reinvestment.
  • Historical suffering has forged a powerful collective resilience and drive to succeed.

After reaching high positions in society, they often use their influence to promote and protect the interests of their community. ğŸ‡¨ğŸ‡¦ Montreal & Quebec City

During my summer internship, a colleague strongly recommended I visit Montreal. Indeed, it’s a city that makes a strong first impression: the French streetscapes, the charm of the Old Port, and the blend of modern architecture and avant-garde art. It has the efficiency of a North American metropolis yet retains a leisurely French rhythm.

Montreal: Trade, Conflict, and Peace

Contrary to popular belief, Montreal truly began to develop after the British took over. During the French rule, Quebec’s political center was Quebec City, and Montreal was just a small trading post.

In the early 17th century, a small group of French settlers arrived here and constantly clashed with the Indigenous peoples. Due to limited support from the French crown, the colonists were not militarily superior. Ultimately, it was the Indigenous peoples who took the initiative to propose a peace treaty to promote trade. The Great Peace of Montreal, signed in 1701, was a rare, relatively equal treaty in North American history that brought decades of peace to the region. It was only later, after diseases brought from Europe decimated the Indigenous population, that French colonists were able to gradually expand.

This history also explains why I saw almost no “Land Acknowledgment” for Indigenous peoples in Quebec, which is common in other parts of Canada. The descendants of French colonists here believe that they were initially focused on trade and did not engage in large-scale land appropriation. Instead, they see themselves as having been conquered by the British, becoming “the ruled” themselves.

Quebec’s “Great Darkness” and “Quiet Revolution”

Before the mid-20th century, Quebec was a very conservative, almost “theocratic” society, a period later known as the “Grande Noirceur” (Great Darkness). Maurice Duplessis, the conservative leader who came to power in 1936, maintained a society deeply dominated by the Catholic Church under the pretext of “protecting French-Canadian culture.” Education, healthcare, and labor unions were all under its influence.

It wasn’t until after Duplessis’s death in the 1960s that Quebec experienced the “Quiet Revolution” (RÃ©volution tranquille)â€”a peaceful transition to modernity. The government took back control of education and healthcare, weakened the church’s influence, promoted industrialization and public services, and established a secular education system. The cultural confidence and identity of Quebec today were established during this period.

Observations on French, Colonialism, and Multiculturalism

I never quite understood why Canada places so much importance on the French language. In fact, the country’s prototype was born from a French colony, and by the time it was seized by Britain in 1763, it already had a rich French-speaking community. The coexistence and interplay between the English and French descendants form Canada’s national narrative of “two founding peoples,” which extends to today’s institutional guarantees of multiculturalism.

Preserving the French language is not just about cultural diversity; it’s about maintaining this historical narrative. Otherwise, Canada would be culturally almost indistinguishable from the United States. This also explains why the government insists on bringing in French-speaking immigrants through various programs, even if many just see it as a shortcut to immigration. The political significance behind this far outweighs the economic considerations. It’s like how the CCP absolutely needs the unification of both sides of the strait.

Of course, this policy also creates real-world conflicts. Quebec’s language laws require business signs to be predominantly in French. The most famous example is KFC being forced to change its name to PFK (Poulet Frit Kentucky). The push and pull between protecting French and promoting economic openness is an ongoing issue for Quebec and all of Canada.

Furthermore, unlike Spanish-style colonial rule, the relationship between Indigenous peoples and colonists in North America was not simply one of invader and invaded; it was a history rich with interaction and fusion. These relationships were often driven by trade, geopolitics, and inter-tribal rivalries, full of complex alliances and maneuvering.

For example, in the early 17th century, French explorer Samuel de Champlain, to secure the lucrative fur trade, formed military alliances with Indigenous tribes like the Huron-Wendat and the Algonquin. The French used firearms to help them fight against their traditional enemy, the powerful Iroquois Confederacy. In response, the Iroquois Confederacy sided first with the Dutch and later with the British, creating a standoff that lasted for over a century.

These alliances were not static. Indigenous tribes were highly autonomous political entities that chose which European power to cooperate with based on their own interests. This complex interaction forms an equally important narrative thread in the history of North American colonization.

ğŸ‡¨ğŸ‡¦ Ottawa

After a five-day stay in Quebec, I flew to Canada’s capital, Ottawa. It is located on the border between Ontario and Quebec and was chosen as the capital in the mid-19th century for its neutral geographical location, to mediate the rivalry between the two major cities of the time, Toronto and Montreal.

As soon as I landed, the atmosphere of Ottawa as a political center hit me: wide streets, neat buildings, and government institutions everywhere. This sense of order is also reflected in its safety; Ottawa’s crime rate is much lower than that of other major North American cities like Toronto and Vancouver.

It’s worth mentioning that the concept of “United Nations Peacekeeping Forces” was first proposed by then-Canadian Secretary of State for External Affairs, Lester B. Pearson, during the 1956 Suez Crisis, for which he was awarded the 1957 Nobel Peace Prize.

Parliament Hill is undergoing a renovation that will last for more than a decade, so I couldn’t go inside. The Centennial Flame in front of the building was lit in 1967 to commemorate the 100th anniversary of the Canadian Confederation. It has been burning ever since, symbolizing the nation’s unity and vitality.

Across the river from Parliament Hill is the Canadian Museum of History. Its location is ingenious: the museum itself is in Gatineau, Quebec, directly opposite Parliament Hill in Ontario. This layout symbolizes the dialogue and balance between Canada’s two founding “nations,” the English and the French, and provides an excellent window into how Canada officially narrates its own history.

The museum offers a comprehensive look at the evolution of this land from the time of the Indigenous peoples, through the fur trade of the Hudson’s Bay Company, to the modern multicultural society. Its detailed content made it a perfect end to my journey.

ğŸ‡ºğŸ‡¸ Chicago

After a week’s rest in Vancouver, I flew to Chicago without stopping.

Chicago truly lives up to its name as the “birthplace of the skyscraper.” The Great Chicago Fire of 1871 razed almost the entire city to the ground, but it also gave city planners and architects a blank slate. The subsequent rebuilding led to a global architectural revolution, giving rise to the world’s first skyscrapers. Today, the downtown area is filled with towering buildings of diverse styles, like an open-air architectural museum.

The city also exudes a strong industrial atmosphere. The steel structures, elevated railways, and brick buildings seen everywhere on the streets speak to its history as the industrial heart of the American Midwest. In the late 20th century, Chicago successfully transformed into a global center for finance, technology, and culture, but its industrial heritage has been well-preserved, becoming a unique part of the city’s cultural landscape.

There are many places and statues named after Lincoln in the city. Although Lincoln was not born here, he has a deep connection with Chicago. It was at the Republican National Convention held in Chicago in 1860 that Lincoln was nominated as the presidential candidate, which began his path to becoming one of America’s greatest presidents.

After a brief two-day stay in Chicago, I arrived in Puerto Rico in the Caribbean. It is an unincorporated territory of the United States. Residents have U.S. citizenship but only have a non-voting representative in Congress and cannot participate in presidential elections.

San Juan, the capital of Puerto Rico, is also one of the oldest European colonial cities in the Americas, founded by the Spanish in 1525â€”making it the oldest city in U.S. territory.

I encountered an interesting phenomenon when I took a taxi from the airport: every time Uber dispatched a car, without exception, the driver was a woman. This was also largely true for the rest of my trip. After a little research, I found that while the female labor force participation rate in Puerto Rico is lower than in the mainland U.S., women are unusually active in the gig economy, such as ride-sharing. This might be related to local family structures and the demand for flexible work hours. The proportion of female drivers here is about 20% higher than the U.S. mainland average, which is interesting.

The most famous historical sites here are the fortifications that the Spanish spent centuries building, considered the strongest in the Caribbean. Starting in the 16th century, to protect this “key” connecting the Old and New Worlds, the Spanish built Castillo San Felipe del Morro and Castillo San CristÃ³bal. In the centuries that followed, they successfully repelled attacks from the British Navy (1595), the Dutch fleet (1625), and numerous pirates and great powers. They were battle-tested fortresses (in contrast, the forts I visited earlier in Halifax and Quebec City never saw real combat ğŸ¤£). Even during the Spanish-American War in 1898, the fortresses were not breached.

The colonial architectural style of the old town has been perfectly preserved, and there’s actually a “big hand” of unified planning behind it. The U.S. National Park Service and federal historic preservation agencies have strict regulations for the restoration of the old town, requiring approval for everything from building colors to restoration materials. This carefully crafted “city brand” can be seen in many historic cities in the U.S., and thanks to good aesthetic sense, the result is not out of place.

Colonialism, Civilization, and Dehumanization

This was my first time in the Caribbean. Today, Puerto Rico’s per capita GDP is about half that of the U.S. mainland. Its standard of living is far better than most Latin American countries, but the gap is still significant.

As a former Spanish colony, this place also made me ponder a phenomenon: why have the vast majority of former Spanish colonies (the countries of Latin America) struggled on the path to modernization, often falling into structural imbalances, while many former British colonies (like the U.S., Canada, Australia, Singapore), despite their own cruel and bloody histories, managed to establish relatively stable modern nations? Some say it’s because they completely slaughtered the indigenous populations, but the Spanish were even more brutal in their slaughter, yet failed to establish a better model.

Historians often compare two different colonial models: Spanish colonial rule was more of a “resource extraction” type. The societies it established were hierarchical with highly concentrated power, designed to funnel the wealth of the New World back to the mother country. The institutional legacy it left behind was often oligarchy and economic dependence.

British colonization, especially in North America, was more focused on “settler societies.” It transplanted a whole system including common law, private property rights, and parliamentary institutions, which laid the foundation for later capitalist development.

Of course, history is far more complex than this simple division. The education I received from a young age accustomed me to seeing colonizers as purely oppressorsâ€”even as completely dehumanized, brutal “monsters.” But now I’m gradually realizing that simply labeling historical individuals as “evil” is itself a form of intellectual laziness, a reverse “dehumanization.” This perspective ignores the immense shaping power of institutions, environment, and the prevailing ideas of the time on individuals.

Ironically, the reason colonizers were able to commit such atrocities was precisely because of their “dehumanizing” view of the colonized indigenous peoplesâ€”seeing them as backward, ignorant “others” who could be dominated at will. It was by stripping them of their “humanity” that exploitation and enslavement became justifiable. This is also the root of many historical tragedies.

Of course, the ideas and behaviors we take for granted today might, to people hundreds of years from now, also contain some blind spots and “dehumanization” that we are unaware of.
